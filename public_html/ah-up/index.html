Ah-Up! Midterm Project

Herbert Li and Hanming Zeng

Introduction:

Ah-Up is a voice-controlled game inspired by other vertical platformers like Doodle Jump. In Ah-Up, you take control of rocket and use your voice to propel it upwards while grabbing coins and avoiding obstacles. You can play the game on the left (if you don't see it, make sure you're using HTTPS).

How to Play:

In regular mode (pressing ‘S’ in the start screen), you can use the A and D keys to move left and right, but you need to use your voice to make the rocket move up and down. In cheat mode (pressing ‘C’ in the start screen), you can just use the WASD keys to control the rocket in addition to your voice. The goal of the game is to fly as high as possible while avoiding comets, collecting coins, and making sure not to fall under the Danger Line. It’s a great stress reliever because it makes use of your desperate screams. It’s also fun to play with your friends, which you can do constructively (both of you trying to win), or destructively (having your friend try and sabotage you by yelling).

Implementation Details:

For this project, we defined several classes: Rocket, Coin, Obstacle, Particle, etc. For collisions, we used the extremely useful library, p5.collide2D (we recommend anyone who needs collision detection in their project to use this library, it's super easy).

Background scrolling:

We used a very long picture for our background and just scrolled it down during the game play. When it runs out, it just loops back to initial state.

Particle System:

For this, we made a Particle class. In its constructor, you can specify its x-position, y-position, and whether the particle should fall up or down. The particles also reduce their opacity over time. Whenever a particle goes out of bounds or its opacity reaches 0, it is removed in order to save memory.

Animation:

Here we made use of a separate game state. In this state, we continued to draw the coins and obstacles as normal, but we removed the collision and voice detection for the rocket in order to make it fall, and rotated it to make it seem like it was spinning out of control.

Moving Forward:

Both of us are not designers, so given more time we would've liked to improve our UI and add custom assets/animations for the various objects in our game. Another idea that we wanted to incorporate was to use the voice recognition library in p5. One idea was for the user to say "save me" in order to continue playing, or saying "shoot" in order to destroy obstacles. We also thought about using cookies to save high scores. Of course, if we had time we could also run a backend DB to store user score data.

Challenges:

Our first challenge came very early on. Although the rocket moves upwards, we found it easier to make everything fall downwards instead. A second challenge was collision detection, which we solved through the use of p5.collide2D. The death animation was also a challenge (though more annoying than challenging) as we had to add an additional state to the game.
